InkTank

InkTank is a mobile application I designed with the tattoo community in mind. I produced this over six months as a way for me to sharpen my UX skills and prowess.

User Personas

When designing an experience, it's imperative to think about context. That's why I created user personas based on real-world data. These are imaginary people with traits and attitudes from real people. And not just any people! I researched tattoo artists, tattoo enthusiasts, and casuals. I was now able to create personas that InkTank would be designed for. You will meet Janice, who is going to be the example for this case study. When I got stuck or was second-guessing myself, I asked, "What would Janice want? What would she think about this"?

The Big Problem

With personas in mind, I refined the problem list and extracted a few of the most important ones. This allowed me to build a problem statement.
Making a problem statement helps clearly define what I'm up against. Now I can outline my solution and get started on a prototype.
Janice needs a pain free way to find a tattoo artist that will make her a piece she will always love because she doesn't know a lot about tattoo styles and feels overwhelmed. We will know this to be true when Janice uses our app to find both what styles she likes and an artist who can create a beautiful tattoo she will love forever.
The best solution will be an app where Janice can select picture examples of styles she likes and doesn't like. Then she'll be able to browse local artists in that style, check on reviews and chat with them to see if they're a good fit.

Site Map

I created a sitemap to help me understand how users would navigate InkTank. This also helps serve as the basis for the user flows, which you'll see next.

User Flows

As a first-time customer, Janice wants to find an artist that will make her comfortable and do a good job, so she can be confident in her first tattoo.

Low Fidelity Wireframes

Janice's user persona and task flow help guide the design process and prioritize features as well. Designing with someone in mind is crucial to success and efficiency during the process. The first version of the home page had a suggested artist for the user upon opening the app. For a few reasons, I redesigned this completely in later iterations.

Medium Fidelity Wireframes

I redesigned the home page to feature a feed of tattoo content from other users. I wanted users to have the power to sort by people and artists they follow, by local artists and posts, or suggested content. I initially was using a red, white, and grey scheme for simplicity and visibility.

User Testing

Now that I had a solid working prototype, I wanted to begin testing my ideas and refining the design.
I recruited five participants, giving them tasks and scenarios. I like giving out scenarios to help put InkTank into a real-world context, and it can help participants understand how the app is ideally supposed to be used.

Usability Tests

  1. Create an account
  2. Search for balck and white tattoo artists
  3. Submit a new tattoo idea.

I ran five tests, with a mix of in-person and remote testing. I recorded the screen and computer audio, and in remote sessions, I used zoom screen share to view the participant's screen. Due to the COVID-19 19 pandemic, I only conducted in-person tests with people in my quarantine bubble.
In total, I uncovered five specific errors thanks to the test sessions. These pain points ranged from medium to high severity, so fixing them was critically important for InkTank's success. Perhaps the most important error was the instance where one participant could not submit the tattoo idea. This was catastrophic because if users can't submit their ideas to local artists, InkTank doesn't offer artists something they're missing; Marketing, networking, and booking all in one platform.

Preference Test

Equipment- UsabilityHub Screens tested:

  • Splash/Introduction Screen
  • Sign Up form screen

17 total participants
Over 70% of participants preferred the darker splash screen. According to UsabilityHub, the difference is likely to be 95% statistically significant.
Using the significant results and the user feedback, I quickly updated our high-fidelity wireframes. The lighter background color is easier on the eyes, and the signup form now has a lighter background color. I applied this to each screen.

Accessibility

Many of the accessibility issues found existed across multiple screens. Among the most common were poor contrasts, small text, small buttons, and text forms without placeholders.
